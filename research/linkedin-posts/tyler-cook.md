### Post 1: The 31 "Rules" of Email Marketing for 2025

- Date: December 31, 2024
- Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/cook-tyler_31-rules-of-email-marketing-for-2025-activity-7280268012656513024-pCcO
- Topic: Comprehensive email marketing principles, B2B SaaS email strategy
- Summary:
Tyler shares 31 actionable rules for email marketing in 2025, covering content strategy, list management, deliverability, and psychological principles. The rules emphasize serving buyers first, ignoring most "best practices" in favor of testing, and treating open rates as directional metrics only.

- Key ideas:
Write interesting content and sell what your list wants to buy
Send more better emails, not less — quality volume matters
Build a sizzling hot list — lukewarm leads are death by 1,000 cuts
Write to serve your buyers first, everyone else second
Write well enough that you don't need images
Email algorithms are personal first and know more than you
Ignore 90% of "best practices" — test and come to your own conclusions
Regularly scrub your list; email mistakes compound extremely quickly
Open rates are ONLY a directional metric and should not dictate strategy
The right list segments will save your list (so will suppression lists)
Encourage replies and other engagement often
Write with an opinion; avoid duplicate content
Encourage unsubscribes (lukewarm leads hurt more than unsubscribes help)
Write to one ideal person (preferably a buyer)
Not everything needs to be automated — a little friction can go a long way
Subject lines aren't as important as you think
Poor deliverability has outsized effects

- Why this matters for B2B SaaS email / newsletter research:
This is Tyler's most comprehensive strategic framework. Rule #4 ("Write to serve your buyers first") is critical for SaaS newsletters where the goal is retention and expansion, not just acquisition. Rule #9 corrects a common mistake — optimizing for opens instead of outcomes. Rule #15 ("Not everything needs automation") is contrarian but valuable: some manual touch points create higher-quality engagement than automated sequences.

- Key quote: "Email algorithms are personal first and know more than you. Ignore 90% of 'best practices.' Test and come to your own conclusions."

## Post 2: The Bounce Management System — Saving 10-15% of Your List

- Date: April 24, 2024
- Link: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/cook-tyler_emailmarketing-emailmarketingstrategy-activity-7189363501851721728-_rqN
- Topic: List recovery, bounce management, multi-channel subscriber retention

- Summary:
Tyler reveals an overlooked email strategy: bounce management as a list-saving system. Businesses lose 25-30% of their email list annually due to job changes, expired emails, and bad addresses. By tracking bounces and following up on secondary channels (LinkedIn, X, Instagram), Tyler saves 10-15% of subscribers who would otherwise be lost forever.

- Key ideas:
You lose roughly 25-30% of your email list each year — mostly from job changes and bad email addresses
Set up a bounce management automation that triggers internal notifications when an email bounces
Assign a task to reach out on LinkedIn (or another channel your audience uses weekly)
Message template: "Hey [Name], Just tried to send you an email, but it looks like it didn't go through. Is [EMAIL] still a good one for you?"
Three possible responses: confirmation (start a conversation), updated email (update ESP and engage), or silence
Choose a channel your audience uses at least once per week — if they don't check it, they'll miss your message

- Why this matters for B2B SaaS email / newsletter research:
For B2B SaaS, where email lists often contain corporate addresses that change when buyers switch jobs, this strategy directly addresses list decay. The 10-15% recovery rate means thousands of preserved subscribers for growing SaaS companies. More importantly, the follow-up conversation on LinkedIn often opens sales discussions that wouldn't have happened otherwise — turning a technical bounce into a revenue opportunity.

- Key quote: "First — you lose roughly 25-30% of your email list each year. Usually due to job changes, getting a new email address, old emails going bad, etc. What I'm about to teach you will help you keep 10-15% of those subscribers."
